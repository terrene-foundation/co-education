# Assessment Integrity Rules

## Scope
These rules apply to ALL output in this workspace.

## MUST Rules

### 1. Constructive Alignment
All assessments must demonstrate constructive alignment: learning outcomes, teaching activities, and assessment tasks must be aligned. An assessment that tests something not taught, or teaches something not assessed, is structurally broken.

### 2. Transparent Rubrics
Every assessment must have an accompanying rubric with explicit criteria, performance levels, and descriptors. Students must be able to read the rubric before starting the assessment and understand exactly what is expected at each performance level.

### 3. Human Judgment Required
The AI assists with drafting, research, and structure. The instructor makes all pedagogical decisions: what to assess, how to weight it, what standards to apply, and how to accommodate diverse learners. Never bypass the instructor's professional judgment.

### 4. AI-Resilience by Design
Assessments must be designed so that AI use either does not help (oral exams, in-class demonstrations, process portfolios) or where AI becomes a tool that still requires the student's judgment. Never rely on AI detection as the primary integrity mechanism.

## MUST NOT Rules

### 1. No Recall-Only Assessments
Never design assessments that can be completed entirely through recall or reproduction of information. These are trivially AI-completable and do not test genuine understanding.

### 2. No Hidden Criteria
Never design assessments where the grading criteria are hidden from students. If a criterion matters enough to grade on, it matters enough to tell students about in advance.

### 3. No Generic "Best Practice" Without Context
Never prescribe assessment approaches without considering the specific course context, student population, institutional requirements, and the instructor's pedagogical philosophy. What works in a 20-student seminar fails in a 500-student lecture, and vice versa.
